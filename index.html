Methodology

Vehicle Dynamics
Equations.

The braking system is engineered bottom-up using vehicle dynamics first principles. Each parameter feeds into the next — from pedal force through hydraulics, to rotor torque, to stopping distance. Below is the complete calculation chain used in this design.

01
Pedal Ratio & Hydraulic Pressure
Pedal Ratio (Mechanical Leverage)
PR = L2 / L1 = 12 / 1.5 = 8

The pedal acts as a lever. L2 is the distance from the pivot to the foot pad; L1 is the distance from pivot to the master cylinder pushrod. A ratio of 8 means every 1N of foot force generates 8N at the master cylinder input.

L2 = 12 inch (pivot → foot) L1 = 1.5 inch (pivot → MC) PR = 8 (dimensionless)
Total Force to Master Cylinder (Fbp)
Fbp = Fd × PR = 300 × 8 = 2400 N

The amplified hydraulic input force delivered to the master cylinder bore. This is the mechanical force before hydraulic conversion.

Fd = 300 N (driver foot force) Fbp = 2400 N
Result: 2400 N applied to master cylinder piston
Hydraulic Pressure in System (P)
P = Fbp / Amc = 2400 / 298.65 = 8.04 N/mm²

Pascal's law converts the piston force into fluid pressure that is transmitted equally through all brake lines to each caliper. The master cylinder bore area is calculated from its diameter.

Amc = π × (b/2)² = π × (19.5/2)² = 298.65 mm²
b = 19.5 mm (bore dia) Amc = 298.65 mm² P = 8.04 N/mm²
02
Caliper Clamping & Rotor Torque
Clamping Force at Caliper (Fmax)
Fmax = P × Acal × μ = 8.04 × 298.65 × 0.45 = 2160 N

The hydraulic pressure acts on the caliper piston area to generate a clamping force on the brake pad. The friction coefficient (μ) between pad and disc converts this clamp load into a friction force that creates braking torque.

P = 8.04 N/mm² Acal = 298.65 mm² μ = 0.45 (pad-disc friction) Fmax = 2160 N
Effective Disc Radius (Re)
Re = (OD + ID) / 4 = (190 + 180) / 4 = 92.5 mm

The effective radius is the mean radius at which the brake pad contacts the disc. It lies between the inner and outer disc diameters. This is the moment arm for torque calculation.

OD = 190 mm (outer diameter) ID = 180 mm (inner diameter) Re = 92.5 mm = 0.0925 m
Braking Torque on Rotor (Trotor)
Trotor = Fmax × Re = 2160 × 0.0925 = 199.8 Nm

The torque generated at the disc rotor by the caliper clamping force acting at the effective radius. This torque acts to resist wheel rotation and decelerates the vehicle.

Fmax = 2160 N Re = 0.0925 m Trotor = 199.8 Nm
Result: 199.8 Nm of braking torque per axle
03
Braking Force & Deceleration
Braking Force at Tyre Contact Patch (Fb)
Fb = Trotor / Rt

The rotor torque is converted into a longitudinal braking force at the tyre contact patch by dividing by the tyre rolling radius. This is the actual retarding force acting on the road surface.

Fb_front = 199.8 / 0.25 = 799.2 N  |  Fb_rear = 799.2 × 2 = 1598.4 N
Rt = 0.25 m (tyre radius) Front = 799.2 N Rear total = 1598.4 N Total Fb = 2397.6 N
Vehicle Deceleration (a)
a = Ft / M = 2397.6 / 2000 = 1.199 m/s²

Newton's second law applied to the braking event. Total braking force divided by vehicle mass gives the deceleration. Note: the required deceleration from load transfer analysis is higher — this gap identified the need for design optimisation.

Ft = 2397.6 N (total braking force) M = 2000 kg a = 1.199 m/s²
Stopping Distance (s)
s = v² / (2 × a) = 13.89² / (2 × 1.199) = 80.5 m

Using kinematics, the stopping distance from initial velocity to rest. v = 50 km/h = 13.89 m/s. This value was used to evaluate compliance against the required braking performance target.

v = 13.89 m/s (50 km/h) a = 1.199 m/s² s = 80.5 m
04
Dynamic Load Transfer & Axle Bias
Static Axle Loads
Wf_static = W × b / Wb  |  Wr_static = W × a / Wb

The static load distribution between front and rear axles depends on the centre of gravity (CG) position relative to each axle. 'a' is the CG-to-rear distance, 'b' is CG-to-front distance.

Wf = 17640 × 1.435 / 2.18 = 11615 N  (615 kg)
Wr = 17640 × 0.745 / 2.18 = 6025 N  (1185 kg)
Wb = 2.18 m a = 0.745 m b = 1.435 m W = 17640 N (1800 kg)
Dynamic Load Transfer During Braking
ΔW = M × a_dec × hcg / Wb

During braking, inertia shifts weight forward onto the front axle. The amount of load transferred depends on vehicle mass, deceleration rate, CG height, and wheelbase. This is why front brakes always require more braking force than rears.

ΔW = 1800 × 0.899 × 0.4526 / 2.18 = 336 N  (34.3 kg)
hcg = 0.4526 m a_dec = 0.899 m/s² ΔW = 336 N transferred to front
Required Braking Force per Axle
Freq_f = μ_tg × Wf_dynamic  |  Freq_r = μ_tg × Wr_dynamic

The required braking force on each axle is limited by the tyre-ground friction coefficient (μ = 0.8 for off-road). Exceeding this causes wheel lock-up. This determines the ideal brake force bias.

Freq_f = 0.8 × 8958 = 7166 N  |  Freq_r = 0.8 × 8682 = 6945 N
μ_tg = 0.8 (tyre-ground) Front load = 8958 N (dynamic) Rear load = 8682 N (dynamic)
Bias: 50.8% front / 49.2% rear — near-ideal for this vehicle CG
05
Thermal Analysis — Heat Generation
Kinetic Energy to Heat Conversion
KE = ½ × M × v² = ½ × 2000 × 13.89² = 192,901 J

During an emergency stop, all kinetic energy of the vehicle is converted into heat at the brake disc. This represents the total thermal energy the disc must absorb and dissipate in a single stop event.

M = 2000 kg v = 13.89 m/s KE = 192,901 J
Disc Temperature Rise (ΔT)
ΔT = KE / (m_disc × Cp) = 192901 / (0.9 × 586) = 365.8 °C

The temperature rise of the disc using the specific heat capacity of cast iron (Cp = 586 J/kg·K). This assumes all heat is absorbed by the disc with no convective dissipation — a conservative worst-case assumption.

m_disc = 0.9 kg Cp = 586 J/kg·K (cast iron) ΔT = 365.8 °C
Final Disc Temperature
T_final = T_initial + ΔT = 30 + 365.8 = 395.8 °C

The peak disc temperature under a worst-case emergency stop from 50 km/h. This is well within the operating limit of standard cast iron brake discs (~600°C), confirming the material selection is adequate for the application.

T_initial = 30°C T_final = 395.8°C Within safe limit ✓
Validated: Peak disc temp 395.8°C — within cast iron operating limit of 600°C
